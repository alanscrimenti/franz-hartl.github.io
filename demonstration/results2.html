Results

Presented with this story, most subjects say that Jake killed his aunt intentionally.

This result may seem puzzling, given that Jake clearly did not have the skill to perform the behavior in reliable fashion. Here again, the agent's success owes a great deal to luck, but now people say that the behavior is intentional.

Why does this occur? One plausible hypothesis would be that people's judgements are in some way influenced by the moral qualities of Jake's behavior, i.e., that people only think Jake acted intentionally because killing one's aunt is so clearly immoral.

We can test this hypothesis by checking to see how people respond when we replace "killing the aunt" with some other outcome that seems less clearly immoral. For example:

Jake desperately wants to win the rifle contest. He knows that he will only win the contest if he hits the bulls-eye. He raises the rifle, gets the bull's-eye in the sights, and presses the trigger.

But Jake isn't very good at using his rifle. His hand slips on the barrel of the gun, and the shot goes wild…

Nonetheless, the bullet lands directly on the bull's-eye. Jake wins the contest..

In fact, when subjects are presented with this modified story, most say that Jake did not intentionally hit the bull's-eye. (show statistics)

This result seems to indicate that moral considerations truly do influence people's judgements as to whether or not the agent acted intentionally.

For further details on this experiment, you can read:

    Knobe, J. (forthcoming). Intentional Action in Folk Psychology: An Experimental Investigation. Philosophical Psychology.
