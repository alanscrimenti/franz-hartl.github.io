Results

Presented with this story, most subjects say that Frank doesn't think Jerry hit the triple 20 intentionally.

The key issue here seems to be that, even though Jerry was clearly trying to hit the triple 20, he didn't have the skill necessary to reliably transform that trying into a successful action. His success owes too much to luck.

Subjects respond very differently when presented with a vignette in which Jerry seems to have the skill to hit the triple 20 in a fairly reliable fashion:

Jerry and Frank are playing darts. Jerry is a novice; he has never played darts before and is not particularly talented at games like this.

Frank explains to Jerry that he can get the most points if he
hits the triple 20, the small field shown below.



Then they start playing. Jerry says, ``Okay, so I go for the triple 20? I don't know how to do it, but I'm gonna try.'' Lo and behold, Jerry hits the triple 20 on his first throw.

Frank comments, ``Classic beginner's luck.''

Jerry gets angry and says, ``What do you mean, luck? I wanted to hit it.''

But Frank counters, ``Sure you wanted, but it was still luck. All right, show me. Hit it again!''

So Jerry throws another dart and again hits the triple 20.

Confronted with this latter vignette, the majority of subjects say that Frank thinks Jerry hit the triple 20 intentionally. (show statistics)

Based on the results of this experiment and a number of others, Bertram Malle and I concluded that people's concept of intentional action includes a skill component.

At the time, we thought that people would generally be unwilling to say that an agent acted intentionally if they thought that the agent lacked the requisite skill.

For more details see:

    Malle, B. F. & Knobe, J. (1997). The Folk Concept of Intentionality. Journal of Experimental Social Psychology, 33, 101-121.

I now think that the view we put forth in this 1997 paper was not quite right. The chief difficulty will become clear in the next experiment.
