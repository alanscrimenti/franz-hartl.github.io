Explaining the Results

Why do we obtain these results? When we switch the outcome from good to bad, why do people suddenly have different intuitions about whether or not the behavior was performed intentionally?

A number of hypotheses seem plausible. Let me present just one.

Perhaps, when people are wondering whether a behavior is intentional, they ask themselves whether the person deserves blame or praise for the behavior. That is, they ask themselves whether the person stands to the behavior in a relation such he deserves praise if the behavior is good and blame if the behavior is bad.

Now, if the agent manages to hit the bull's-eye by sheer luck, it seems that the agent doesn't deserve much praise for hitting the bull's-eye. But if the agent hits his aunt by pure luck, he still deserves a great deal of blame for hitting her. And perhaps that's why people say that the agent hit the aunt intentionally but didn't hit the bull's-eye intentionally. (show statistics)

Similar considerations apply to side-effects — i.e., to effects that the agent foresaw but wasn't specifically trying to bring about. If the agent's behavior has a bad side-effect, she will usually be blamed for it. But if the agent's behavior has a good side-effect, she will not usually be praised for it. And perhaps that's why people said that the chairman intentionally brought about the bad side-effect but didn't intentionally bring about the bad side-effect. (show statistics)
